## UNIVERSAL RULES BEFORE YOU SPEAK

1. **Never name a number first** unless forced. Let them anchor.
2. **Never accept on the spot.** Always say: "I'm very excited. I'd like to take 24 hours to review the full package."
3. **Never lie about competing offers.** You will be caught and blacklisted.
4. **Never apologize for negotiating.** Employers expect it. 84% of employers have room to increase an initial offer (Robert Half 2025).
5. **Never negotiate over text/chat.** Phone or video call. Written words lack tone and warmth.
6. **Timing:** Negotiate AFTER you have a written offer. Never before. The offer letter is your leverage.

## SCRIPT 1: COUNTER-OFFERING THE INITIAL OFFER (+15-25%)

### Context
You received an offer of $75,000. Market rate is $85,000-$95,000. You want $90,000.

### What to Say

> "Thank you so much for this offer — I'm genuinely excited about joining [Company] and working with the team on [specific project or goal discussed in interview].
>
> I've done thorough research on market compensation for this role in [city/region], looking at data from Glassdoor, Levels.fyi, and industry benchmarks. For someone with my [X years] of experience and [specific skill/certification], the market range is $85,000 to $95,000.
>
> Given the value I'll bring — specifically my experience in [concrete achievement, e.g., "reducing deployment time by 40% at my current company"] — I'd like to propose a base salary of $90,000. I believe that's fair for both sides and reflects the impact I plan to make.
>
> Is there flexibility to close that gap?"

### What NEVER to Say
- "I need more money because my rent is high." (Personal finances are irrelevant to your market value.)
- "My friend at Google makes $120K." (Irrelevant comparison.)
- "I guess I could accept $75K if that's all you have..." (Signals weakness immediately.)
- "That's way too low." (Adversarial tone kills goodwill.)

### If They Say "We Can't Go That High"

> "I understand budget constraints. Could we explore a $5,000 signing bonus to bridge the gap? Or perhaps a guaranteed salary review at 6 months tied to [specific KPI]? I want to make this work because I'm genuinely excited about this role."

## SCRIPT 2: NEGOTIATING REMOTE WORK

### Context
The offer says "hybrid 3 days in office." You want full remote or 1 day in office.

### What to Say

> "I'm thrilled about this offer and I'm fully committed to [Company]'s success. I wanted to discuss the work arrangement.
>
> In my current role, I've been fully remote for [X years] and my results have actually improved — [specific metric: "I shipped 23% more features" or "my client satisfaction score went from 87 to 94"]. I find I do my deepest, most focused work from my home office.
>
> Would you be open to a primarily remote arrangement — perhaps one day in the office per week for team collaboration and planning? I'm flexible on which day works best for the team.
>
> I want to set myself up to do my best work for you, and I believe this arrangement supports that."

### What NEVER to Say
- "I don't want to commute." (Makes it about you, not them.)
- "Everyone else is going remote these days." (Pressuring with trends annoys managers.)
- "I won't accept if it's not remote." (Ultimatums before you've built trust.)

### If They Say "Our Policy Requires 3 Days"

> "I respect the company policy. Could we try a 90-day pilot where I work 1-2 days in-office, and if my performance metrics are strong, we revisit the arrangement? That way there's zero risk on your side."

## SCRIPT 3: ASKING FOR A SIGNING BONUS

### Context
Salary is acceptable but you want a signing bonus to compensate for leaving unvested equity, a pending bonus, or relocation costs.

### What to Say

> "I'm very happy with the base salary — thank you. There is one thing I'd like to discuss. By joining [Company], I'll be walking away from [choose one: "$8,000 in unvested stock that vests in March" / "my annual bonus that pays out in Q1" / "I'll need to relocate from [city]"].
>
> Would the company consider a one-time signing bonus of $[amount] to help offset that transition cost? This would make my decision straightforward, and I'm ready to commit as soon as we align on this.
>
> I know signing bonuses are common for situations like this, and I want to be transparent about what would make this a clear yes for me."

### What NEVER to Say
- "I deserve a signing bonus." (Entitlement repels.)
- "Other companies offered me a signing bonus." (Unless you have a real written offer — never bluff.)
- "I need it for personal expenses." (Not their problem.)

### Key Numbers
- Typical signing bonuses: $2,000-$10,000 for mid-level, $10,000-$50,000+ for senior/executive.
- Most common: equivalent to 5-10% of base salary.
- Always ask for 20% more than your target — they will negotiate down.

## SCRIPT 4: NEGOTIATING A TITLE UPGRADE

### Context
The offer says "Marketing Coordinator." You want "Marketing Manager" based on the actual scope of the role.

### What to Say

> "I'm excited about the responsibilities — managing a team of 3, owning the content calendar, and reporting directly to the VP. Those responsibilities align closely with a Marketing Manager title rather than Coordinator.
>
> The title matters to me for two reasons: first, it accurately reflects the scope of work you've described, and second, it positions me to be the most effective representative of [Company] when I'm working with external vendors and partners.
>
> Would you be open to adjusting the title to Marketing Manager? The scope of work wouldn't change — it would simply reflect what I'll actually be doing."

### What NEVER to Say
- "Coordinator sounds too junior for me." (Sounds ego-driven.)
- "I need Manager for my LinkedIn." (Trivializes the ask.)
- "I've been a Manager before so I can't go back." (Sounds inflexible.)

### If They Say "Title Is Standard for This Level"

> "I understand the leveling framework. Could we agree on an accelerated title review at 6 months? If I'm performing at the Manager level — which this role scope requires — the title would be updated to match. Would that work?"

## SCRIPT 5: HANDLING "THAT'S OUR FINAL OFFER"

### Context
They've said the salary is non-negotiable. You still have leverage on other terms.

### What to Say

> "I appreciate your transparency, and I respect that the salary is set. I'm still very interested in this role.
>
> Could we explore other parts of the package? Specifically, I'd like to discuss [pick 2-3]:
> - An additional week of PTO (from 15 to 20 days)
> - A professional development budget of $2,000-$3,000 annually
> - A signing bonus to offset my transition
> - A guaranteed salary review at 6 months based on performance milestones
> - Flexible work schedule (e.g., 4x10 or remote Fridays)
> - Equity or stock options if available
>
> I want to make this work. Which of these might have some flexibility?"

### What NEVER to Say
- "Fine, I'll take it then." (You just left thousands on the table. Always explore the full package.)
- "That's disappointing." (Adversarial.)
- "My other offer is higher." (Only say this if it is true and you have it in writing.)

### The Psychology
When someone says "final offer," they mean "final on THIS item." The rest of the package almost always has flexibility. Benefits, PTO, start date, title, review timeline, and bonuses are separate budget lines.

## SCRIPT 6: NEGOTIATING BENEFITS WHEN SALARY IS FIXED

### Context
Government job, union position, or strict pay band. Salary truly cannot change.

### What to Say

> "I understand the salary is fixed within the pay band, and I'm comfortable with that. I'd like to discuss the broader package to make sure we're aligned.
>
> A few things that would be meaningful to me:
>
> 1. **Professional development**: Would the organization support one conference per year and a $2,500 training budget? I want to stay sharp and bring the latest practices back to the team.
> 2. **Flexible schedule**: Is there an option for compressed hours (4x10) or a regular remote day?
> 3. **Start date**: Could I start on [date 2 weeks later than proposed]? I'd like to take a brief break between roles to start fresh and energized.
> 4. **Vacation**: Is there flexibility to start at [X+5] days rather than the standard [X] days, given my [Y years] of experience?
>
> Even one or two of these would make a real difference."

### What NEVER to Say
- "The salary is too low but I'll take it." (Starts the relationship with resentment.)
- "Can you make an exception to the pay band?" (Shows you do not understand their constraints.)

## SCRIPT 7: DECLINING AN OFFER PROFESSIONALLY

### Context
You've decided to decline. You want to preserve the relationship for future opportunities.

### What to Say (Email or Call)

> "Thank you so much for the offer to join [Company] as [Title]. I've given this serious thought, and after careful consideration, I've decided to pursue a different opportunity that more closely aligns with my [career goals / family situation / long-term plans] at this time.
>
> This was not an easy decision — I was genuinely impressed by [specific: the team, the mission, the product, the interview process]. I have enormous respect for what you're building.
>
> I'd love to stay connected. If circumstances change in the future, I would welcome the chance to explore working together again.
>
> Thank you for your time, your generosity throughout the process, and for making this such a positive experience."

### What NEVER to Say
- "I got a better offer." (Burns the bridge.)
- "Your salary was too low." (Insulting, even if true.)
- "I'm going to [competitor]." (Unnecessary detail that can create friction.)
- Nothing at all / ghosting. (Worst possible move. The hiring world is small.)

### Timing
Decline within 48 hours of your decision. The company has other candidates waiting. Speed is respect.

## SCRIPT 8: NEGOTIATING AS ENTRY-LEVEL (NO EXPERIENCE LEVERAGE)

### Context
First job or career changer. You feel you have no leverage. You do.

### What to Say

> "Thank you for this offer — I'm excited to start my career at [Company]. Before I sign, I'd like to have an open conversation about the compensation.
>
> I've researched entry-level [role] salaries in [city/region] using Glassdoor, LinkedIn Salary, and [university career center data if applicable]. The range I'm seeing is $[X] to $[Y]. Your offer of $[Z] is [at the lower end / slightly below] that range.
>
> While I'm early in my career, I bring [pick 2-3]:
> - My [specific coursework, capstone project, or thesis topic] directly relevant to this role
> - My [internship/co-op] experience at [company] where I [specific accomplishment]
> - My [certification, e.g., AWS Cloud Practitioner, Google Analytics] which means I can contribute from day one without ramp-up on [specific tool]
>
> Would there be room to adjust the base to $[target, ~8-12% above offer]? I'm committed to proving my value quickly."

### What NEVER to Say
- "I know I don't have experience, but..." (Never undermine yourself.)
- "I have student loans to pay." (Personal finance is not leverage.)
- "I'll take whatever you offer." (Signals zero self-value.)

### Entry-Level Leverage Points You DO Have
- Relevant internships, co-ops, or projects with measurable results
- Certifications (even free ones from Google, AWS, HubSpot, Coursera)
- Specific technical skills the role requires
- Multiple offers or active interview processes (mention without bluffing)
- Your willingness to commit long-term: "I'm looking to grow here for 3-5 years"

## CULTURAL CONSIDERATIONS (GLOBAL)

### North America (US, Canada)
- Negotiation is expected. 73% of employers expect candidates to negotiate. Not negotiating can signal low confidence.
- Be direct but warm. Data-driven arguments win.

### Europe (UK, Germany, France, Netherlands, Nordics)
- Negotiation is accepted but should be measured. Avoid aggressive American-style tactics.
- In Germany and Netherlands, be precise with numbers. In UK, slightly more indirect.
- Many roles have published pay bands — reference them.

### Middle East (UAE, Saudi Arabia, Qatar)
- Total package matters more than base: housing allowance, transport, schooling, flights home.
- Negotiate the full package, not just salary. "What is the housing allowance?" is a standard question.
- Respect hierarchy — negotiate with HR, not your direct manager.

### South Asia (India, Pakistan, Bangladesh, Sri Lanka)
- Negotiation is normal but often more relationship-driven. Build rapport first.
- CTC (Cost to Company) includes many components — ask for the breakout before negotiating.
- In-hand salary vs. CTC can differ by 30-40%.

### East Asia (Japan, South Korea, China)
- Japan: Rarely negotiate base salary. Benefits, bonus, and title have more room.
- South Korea: Similar to Japan. Seniority-based pay is common. Negotiate signing bonus instead.
- China: Negotiation is common in private sector, especially tech. Be direct.

### Africa (Nigeria, South Africa, Kenya, Egypt)
- Always negotiate. First offers are typically 15-25% below budget.
- Ask about non-salary benefits: transport, health insurance, pension contributions.
- In South Africa, know the BEE (Broad-Based Black Economic Empowerment) context.

### Latin America (Brazil, Mexico, Argentina, Colombia)
- Negotiate warmly and relationally. Cold data-only approaches feel transactional.
- Benefits packages vary enormously — always ask for the full breakdown.
- 13th month salary (aguinaldo) is legally required in many countries — do not "negotiate" for what is already mandated.

### Oceania (Australia, New Zealand)
- Direct, Australian-style negotiation works well. Be straightforward but not aggressive.
- Superannuation (retirement) is mandatory in Australia — negotiate salary ABOVE super.
- Ask about salary packaging and novated leases as additional benefits.

## TIMING CHEAT SHEET

| When | Action |
|------|--------|
| During interview | NEVER discuss salary unless they bring it up first. If asked for expectations: "I'd like to learn more about the full scope before discussing numbers. What's the range budgeted for this role?" |
| After verbal offer | "Thank you! I'm very interested. Could I receive the full offer in writing so I can review the complete package?" |
| After written offer | Take 24-48 hours. Then call (not email) to negotiate. |
| After negotiation | Get the revised offer in writing before accepting. |
| After accepting | STOP negotiating. You accepted. Deliver results and renegotiate at your first review. |
